The Approach

JP Salomon is an independent researcher and technical architect who approaches intelligence, civilisation, and complex systems as questions of structure: what persists when you strip away labels and look at dynamics, constraints, and relationships. His work is shaped by what he calls the engineer’s paradox — the fact that you can build and instrument every component of a system and still fail to explain the emergent whole. For Salomon, the unit of understanding is not “the part”, but the pattern of interaction that makes the parts become something new.

A physics-first obsession sits underneath both his research and his engineering: the belief that capability is downstream of constraints, and that energy, bandwidth, and material limits are not implementation details but the governing laws of the design space. That emphasis on energy thrift and constraint-led thinking guides how he evaluates claims, builds systems, and distinguishes real progress from expensive noise.

That lens runs through a corpus of papers proposing substrate-independent ways to think about cognition: intelligence as relational dynamics; cognition as topology; and the idea that very different systems can share the same underlying mechanism when their structures are equivalent. The goal is not philosophy for its own sake, but operational clarity — frameworks that let you compare minds, models, organisms, and organisations using common structural coordinates.

Alongside the research, he builds complete applications and integrated frameworks that turn these ideas into tools, focusing on emergent behaviour in optimisation systems, topological/relational cognition, and embodied, energy-aware computation.

Before moving into full-time research, he spent over a decade in high-end film and television production as an Assistant Director and unit-level coordinator. That background sharpened a core insight that threads through all his work: large-scale coordination is driven not just by facts, but by narrative architecture — the stories people use to compress reality, coordinate groups, and steer attention and behaviour inside complex systems.

Chapter One

Structural Entropy

The Anatomy of the End

Every civilisation tells itself that its end will be different.

The Romans imagined a last stand at the frontiers, a final clash with the barbarian hordes. The British Empire imagined a dignified sunset, power handed over with legal ceremony and trumpet fanfares. The contemporary West imagines apocalypse as a cinematic event: mushroom clouds on the horizon, bioengineered plagues, rogue AIs waking up one morning and deciding to turn off the lights.

But when the end actually comes, it does not look like any of these stories. It looks like accounts in arrears. It looks like empty maternity wards and crowded nursing homes. It looks like twenty‑somethings who no longer date, and forty‑somethings who no longer have friends. It looks like governments that can print claims on the future but cannot repair a bridge. It looks, above all, like a loss of tension – a loosening of the invisible fibres that once pulled individuals into a shared project.

The argument of this book begins from a simple, uncomfortable premise: civilisations do not primarily die by being conquered from without, but by dissolving from within. The barbarians, the pandemics, the financial crises – these are often the final pushes, not the root causes. Underneath the headlines, a slower, quieter process is at work: a rise in structural entropy.

The hidden constant behind different collapses

Historians have long oscillated between two bad explanations for decline. On one side are the moralists: empires fall because of decadence, luxury, loss of virtue. On the other side are the determinists: empires fall because of drought, barbarian migration, resource depletion, or some other brute external shock. Both capture something. Neither explains why the trajectories of such different societies, across millennia, rhyme so closely.

When General Sir John Glubb sat down in the 1970s to compare the lifespans of empires from Assyria to Britain, he was struck by an eerie regularity. The forms of government varied; the religions varied; the technologies of war and trade varied. Yet again and again, the duration converged on roughly 250 years, about ten generations. In The Fate of Empires, Glubb sketched a sequence of stages: an Age of Pioneers, of Conquest, of Commerce, of Affluence, of Intellect, and finally of Decadence, in which pessimism, materialism, and rancorous internal debate displace the earlier civil virtues.

Glubb's scheme is not physics. There is no divine metronome that strikes at year 251 and topples a superpower. But he recognised something real: large human systems have internal dynamics that tend to push them along similar arcs, regardless of the flags and slogans they fly. Prosperity changes behaviour; rising complexity creates new kinds of fragility; elites expand and then turn on each other; the memory of scarcity and struggle fades. The outward symptoms differ; the underlying pattern repeats.

Historical parallels appear throughout the chapters ahead where they clarify a mechanism. They are not one-to-one analogies; they are recurring shapes that emerge when surplus tightens, complexity overshoots, and coordination costs climb.

This book takes that intuition and asks: what if we tried to formalise it? Not as mystical numerology, but as a thermodynamic process.

The Core Invariant

Before we descend into the mechanics, we must state the governing constraint of this entire analysis: Civilisations do not fail because they choose badly, but because they continue optimising for a regime that no longer exists.

This invariant sits above politics, technology, and culture. It is the reason why reform so often accelerates decline—because the reforms are designed to perfect the old system, not to build the new one.

Civilisation as a thermodynamic machine

A civilisation is not an abstraction. It is a physical machine: billions of bodies moving, eating, mating and working; trillions of decisions flowing through institutions; energy and matter being rearranged into cities, roads, data centres and art. To maintain that intricate order against the background drift of entropy, the system must continually do work – importing energy, making decisions, repairing damage, solving problems.

In its youth, this work is cheap. A small bureaucracy can manage a large, simple frontier; a handful of engineers can dam a river and triple the food supply; a basic legal code can reduce violence and enable trade. Each additional unit of complexity – a new office, a new tax, a new regulation – pays for itself many times over in increased stability and surplus. This is the rising-return phase of complexity.

Over time, however, the easy problems are solved. What remain are pathologies at the margin, special cases, vested interests to manage, second‑order effects to contain. The anthropologist Joseph Tainter describes this as the point where the marginal return on complexity starts to fall. Each additional layer of bureaucracy or technical infrastructure solves a narrower problem, yet demands permanent feeding in the form of money, energy and attention. Eventually, the costs of simply keeping the existing system running begin to consume the surplus that once funded innovation, conquest and art.

Modern financial economies make this process legible in a single number: debt. Debt is how a society pulls problem‑solving capacity from the future into the present. In early, expansive phases, each unit of new debt funds productive investment – roads, factories, educated workers – that generates more than enough surplus to repay principal and interest. In late phases, new debt increasingly goes to servicing old promises: pensions, interest on prior borrowing, the bare minimum of maintenance. Analysts like Lacy Hunt have shown that in advanced economies, the marginal growth generated per unit of additional debt has been grinding toward zero – and in some estimates, has already gone negative.

At that point, the civilisation has become a kind of high‑maintenance machine running on fumes. It can still move, sometimes even sprint, but only by cannibalising its own future.

The Seneca cliff: why ruin is rapid

The Roman philosopher Seneca once remarked that "increases are of sluggish growth, but the way to ruin is rapid." In the modern era, Ugo Bardi and others have turned that intuition into a formal model: the Seneca effect, in which systems that take a long time to build can collapse far faster than they arose.

For readers who want the minimal maths behind the shape claim, see Appendix A.

Plot the population of St. Matthew Island's reindeer, introduced in 1944 onto an Arctic island rich with lichen and free of predators. For years, the herd grew exponentially, eventually reaching more than 6,000 animals. Then, in less than three years, it crashed to 42. Plot Syrian oil production in the early 21st century: a long ramp-up, a plateau, and then a sudden 90 percent collapse in under five years once war and field depletion interacted in a vicious circle. Plot the political stability of the Western Roman Empire: two centuries of relative equilibrium followed by a century of currency debasement, military crisis and rapid disintegration.

These curves are not symmetrical bells. They are cliffs. The reasons are subtle but general. Large systems maintain their apparent stability by deploying buffers, redundancies and control loops. They smooth shocks, draw down reserves, borrow against the future, improvise fixes. To the casual observer, nothing much changes; business continues as usual. Underneath, however, reserves are being drained, options foreclosed, flexibility lost. When the homeostatic machinery finally fails, the accumulated tension releases all at once. Decline is faster than growth because it is powered by the stored potential of everything that was propping the system up.

The argument of this book is that contemporary Western civilisation is on the flat upper ledge of such a Seneca cliff. Growth appears anaemic but positive; markets function; services are delivered; the lights stay on. Yet this apparent stability is increasingly the product of extraordinary measures: zero or negative real interest rates, permanent fiscal deficits, political promises that can only be honoured in debased currency rather than real goods and services. The resilience that once absorbed shocks has been spent.

Awareness is not a control variable

A crucial corollary of the Seneca dynamic is that recognition arrives after the gradient has already flipped. Awareness is not a lever; it is a lagging indicator.

Once maintenance costs exceed surplus, the system cannot “decide” its way back to stability. At most, recognition can influence the mode of descent (default vs inflation, fragmentation vs centralisation), not the fact of descent itself. This reframes responsibility: failure is not primarily moral or intellectual, but temporal. Feedback arrives too late to be acted upon at scale.

Four fractures of a late-stage system

To make this more than a mood, we need a diagnostic. In the chapters that follow, four structural fractures will be traced through history and into the present:

  1. Diminishing returns on complexity, expressed economically as debt saturation and the breakdown of the growth‑for‑complexity bargain.
  2. Demographic inversion, in which societies voluntarily move below replacement fertility and age into a deflationary, fiscally unsustainable pyramid of dependants atop a shrinking productive base.
  3. Social atomisation, the behavioural sink in which individuals withdraw from work, family and community into private, often digital, cocoons, even as material abundance peaks.
  4. Elite dissociation, where the ideology of the ruling class floats free of material and biological reality, crystallising into “luxury beliefs” that signal status for their holders but are ruinous when applied to the lower orders.

These are not separate stories. They are tightly coupled feedback loops. Debt and complexity make it harder to raise children and to form stable families; low fertility amplifies the fiscal burden of old promises; economic stress and cultural narratives encourage retreat into curated, low‑friction digital worlds; elite overproduction channels surplus energy into intra‑elite culture wars and esoteric vocabulary rather than infrastructure and shared purpose.

The result is a civilisation that is, in a precise sense, overbuilt and under‑inhabited. It has more institutional complexity than it can afford, more built infrastructure than it can maintain, more ideological noise than it can integrate. It is architecturally magnificent and demographically hollow.

Late-stage civilisation as phase transition

Physicists describe phase transitions – water freezing, iron becoming magnetised – as points where a system, pushed past a critical threshold, abruptly reorganises its internal order. For a long time, things look linear. Temperature rises and the ice warms, but remains ice. At a particular point, further small increments of heat no longer change the temperature; they go into breaking the crystalline lattice. Then, without any obvious macro‑signal, the structure gives way and the solid becomes liquid.

Late-stage civilisation behaves similarly. For a long time, more debt, more administration, more therapy, more policing, more growth can be poured into the structure without obvious collapse. The graphs kink, but they still slope gently upward. People complain about politics, but food still arrives at the supermarkets. Then, at some not‑obviously‑marked moment, the added energy stops buying stability and starts dissolving it. Institutions lose coherence; norms lose binding power; contradictions that were once papered over become unmanageable.

This is the sense in which the current global order is best understood as being in a state of high‑energy metastability. It is not yet collapsed. But like a superheated liquid or an over‑pressured boiler, it is no longer in a robust equilibrium either. A shock that a younger, less brittle civilisation would absorb – a pandemic, a regional war, an energy crunch – now risks triggering disproportionate cascades.

In the pages ahead, the idea of structural entropy will be developed as a unified way of seeing this condition. Entropy here does not mean “everything gets worse” in a vague moral sense. It means:

  • the loss of usable differences and tensions that once did work – between young and old, core and periphery, elite and mass, male and female, sacred and profane;
  • the smoothing out of life into interchangeable, low‑friction experiences, at the cost of meaning, fertility and resilience;
  • the tendency of complex structures, once the positive returns on further complexity vanish, to decay back toward simpler but harsher forms.

By the time we are done tracing these dynamics through the decline of past empires, the demographic implosion of Europe and East Asia, the behavioural sink of the digital age, and the luxury‑belief labyrinth of contemporary elites, one conclusion will be hard to avoid: our situation is not unique. The technologies are novel; the substrate – human biology, social signalling, thermodynamics – is not.

The task of this first chapter is simply to lay that claim on the table. Civilisation is a heat engine that burns stored order to generate complexity and surplus. For a time, the equation works. Then, step by step, the margins evaporate. What looks from the inside like a series of disconnected crises – pensions, housing, loneliness, ideology – is, from the outside, the anatomy of the end.

In the next chapter, we will slow down and walk Glubb’s 250‑year arc in more detail, matching his stages to historical empires and to the contemporary West. Only then will we begin to climb, with clear eyes, toward the edge of the Seneca cliff.
